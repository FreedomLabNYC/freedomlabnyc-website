Buy Bitcoin P2P Without ID

Workshop Bitcoin Privacy Self-custody

A beginner workshop guide for buying Bitcoin directly from another person, keeping a non-KYC stack separate, and evaluating the tradeoffs of common P2P tools.

What P2P buying means

P2P means peer-to-peer: buying Bitcoin directly from another person instead of a centralized exchange or third party. Bitcoin was designed for this kind of direct settlement, but the modern exchange experience made custodial, KYC-heavy buying feel easier.

P2P trading can reduce custody risk and surveillance, but it is not magic. It shifts responsibility onto you: wallet setup, trade safety, reputation checks, coin control, and careful separation between identity-linked and non-KYC funds.

Why avoid KYC for some Bitcoin

  • KYC is forever: once a purchase record links your ID to Bitcoin, that record can leak, be subpoenaed, or be used years later.
  • Financial privacy matters: businesses, activists, donors, and ordinary people may not want every payment relationship mapped to their identity.
  • Custodial exchanges create targets: exchange databases and purchase histories can expose people to hacks, ransomware, and physical extortion.
  • Capital controls and censorship happen: P2P rails are part of preserving individual and civil-society financial autonomy.

Risks and tradeoffs

  • Premiums: P2P trades often cost 10–20% over spot, depending on payment method and local liquidity.
  • Disputes: escrow helps but does not remove all risk. Understand the dispute process before trading.
  • Counterparty risk: the person you trade with may be dishonest, compromised, or involved in activity you do not want connected to you.
  • Physical safety: cash trades should happen in public, low-risk places with small amounts first.
  • UTXO mistakes: mixing KYC and P2P coins can undo the privacy you were trying to preserve.

Practical options to test

In-person cash

Most private, but it requires trust and physical safety planning. Start small, meet publicly, and avoid revealing your full holdings or long-term wallet structure.

Gift cards bought with cash

Can be useful when direct cash-for-Bitcoin liquidity is scarce. Treat gift-card trades as high-fraud-risk: counterparties often require reputation or extra proof.

Bitcoin ATMs

Some ATMs allow small purchases with only SMS/email, while others require full ID. Check limits, cameras, fees, and verification requirements before treating an ATM as private.

Earn or mine Bitcoin

Earning Bitcoin for work or mining avoids an exchange purchase entirely. It can be operationally harder, but it is often cleaner than trying to buy anonymously later.

Online P2P platforms

Tools like Hodl Hodl, RoboSats, Bisq/Bisq Easy, lnp2pBot, Vexl, LocalCoinSwap, Peach, Paxful/NoOnes-style markets, and regional services all have different privacy, escrow, payment-rail, liquidity, and KYC tradeoffs.

Tool notes from workshop testing

  • Hodl Hodl: email + 2FA account flow; sellers may need to accept a contract before the payment window. Verify currency and payment method with the seller before relying on it.
  • RoboSats: Tor-first, no personal info, Lightning-only. Save the robot token carefully. Coordinator trust matters because coordinators mediate disputes and provide infrastructure.
  • Mostro: Nostr + Lightning P2P exchange infrastructure. It can support local markets when someone runs or joins a node with local liquidity, but do not assume a currency is usable just because the protocol is flexible. Check the visible orderbook and node settings first.
  • Bisq / Bisq 2: stronger decentralization and Tor integration. Bisq 1 requires deposits and desktop uptime; Bisq Easy reduces friction for small buys but uses reputation rather than the same escrow model.
  • Vexl: social-graph/web-of-trust liquidity. It can be powerful if your network has offers, but many users may initially see zero available offers.
  • Peach: escrow-based mobile P2P with regional availability limits, reputation gating, and address-signing requirements under Swiss rules.
  • LocalCoinSwap: account-based marketplace with many payment methods. Some traders or situations may require KYC, screenshots, or other identity/payment proof.
  • lnp2pBot: open-source Telegram + Lightning escrow. Liquidity depends heavily on local communities and channels.
  • ATMs / Unbank-style options: may be convenient for small amounts, but privacy depends on local limits, cameras, phone/email requirements, and operator policy.

HRF and freedom-tech apps to investigate

Some of the most relevant options are not generic global exchanges. They are freedom-tech projects or local-market tools that may need a community, operator, or liquidity maker before they work in a specific country. Treat this list as a starting map, then verify local currency support before teaching or relying on it.

  • Mostro: HRF-aligned Nostr + Lightning P2P exchange infrastructure. Use the Mostro Android releases to test the app, check mostro.community for public nodes, and use the Mostro server repo if you need to bootstrap a local node. Evidence standard: a visible local-currency orderbook beats “the protocol can support it.”
  • SudaBit / Sudan Hodl: Sudan-specific HRF Bitcoin Development Fund lead for private, self-custodial Bitcoin access from local currency. Current public links: SudaBit site and Sudan Hodl on X. Verify whether SDG liquidity is live before presenting it as operational.
  • Banxaas: HRF-covered West Africa Lightning/mobile-money bridge. Public code is at github.com/banxaas/banxaas. Do not assume Sierra Leone support from “West Africa”: verify the exact country, mobile-money rail, and currency. Prior checks pointed to CFA/XOF-region relevance rather than SLE/SLL.
  • Vexl and lnp2pBot: not guaranteed by geography, but useful where local trusted networks or Telegram communities already exist. They are best treated as community-liquidity tools, not universal orderbooks.

Best practices

  1. Separate your stacks: keep KYC and P2P Bitcoin in separate wallets. Do not later merge them casually.
  2. Use self-custody: receive into a wallet where you control the keys, not a platform wallet.
  3. Generate a fresh address for every receipt.
  4. Learn coin control: understand UTXOs, doxxic change, and how wallet coin selection can link funds.
  5. Use Tor or a VPN where appropriate: reduce network-level leakage, especially with P2P web tools.
  6. Check reputation and start small: never make your first trade with a new tool or person a large trade.
  7. Document the trade privately: keep receipts/screenshots needed for disputes, but do not overshare identity information.

Workshop flow

  1. Introductions and quick poll: who owns Bitcoin, has self-custody, or has bought P2P?
  2. Explain P2P, KYC, privacy, custody, and why people choose this tradeoff.
  3. Review risks: premiums, scams, address blacklists, UTXO mistakes, and physical safety.
  4. Compare options: cash, gift cards, ATMs, earning/mining, and online P2P tools.
  5. Demo one or two tools live with small amounts, after testing them beforehand.
  6. Close with wallet hygiene: separate stacks, fresh addresses, Tor/VPN, reputation checks, and coin control.
